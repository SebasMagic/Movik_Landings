A short guide to getting your cash back under control: your reserve, your money, and numbers you can actually read.

Switching factors scares people for one reason: the day you drop one and the new one hasn't kicked in, you run out of cash. So most people put up with it and keep paying charges that don't add up, year after year. This guide gets you out without that gap — you get your reserve back, you close your contract properly, and you keep getting paid with no dead day in between.

03 How to give notice, properly

Almost every contract asks for written notice, typically 30 to 60 days ahead. Do it in writing and keep a copy. Giving notice on time is the difference between a clean exit and getting locked in for another term.

Putting it in writing isn't distrust, it's your backup. That way nobody changes the rules halfway through, and you have the dates to prove it.

04 What happens to your held reserve

Your reserve is your money, just parked. When you leave, the factor releases it after collecting the invoices it already had in hand. Ask in writing for the date you get it back and how. Don't let it sit there.

05 The "dead day": the real fear, and how it disappears

The dead day is that gap between the last invoice your old factor pays and the first one you collect with the new one. That's what actually holds you back. The fix is to leave no gap: have your new provider start collecting your live invoices while the old one closes out its own.
